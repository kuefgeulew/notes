Five days of Durga Puja in an ancestral house. Thirty years of everything else underneath.

One of Rituparno Ghosh's most underrated works. It still deserves more than it got.
Nuance through simplicity
"একদিন তো ভুল করা সব শেষ হয়ে যাবে, তখন তো ঠিকটার কাছে না ফিরে আর উপায় নেই।"
One day, all the mistakes will be over. And then there will be no choice but to return to what is right.
A line from the film · the one that stays
It is not a dramatic line. It is not delivered with music swelling behind it. It is said the way such things are actually said — in passing, between two people who have too much history to need volume. And that, in a single breath, is what Rituparno Ghosh understood about family that most filmmakers never do.
The setup
A joint family, a Durga Puja, and everything underneath
Utsab is about a joint Bengali family coming together in their old ancestral home for Durga Puja. That is the setup. But stating the setup of a Rituparno Ghosh film is like describing weather — technically accurate, entirely insufficient.
What actually happens is this: five days of puja in an ekannoborti household become the surface on which years of unfinished conversations, buried resentments, failed love stories, fraying marriages, financial anxieties, and the quiet grief of growing apart all rise up together. The mechanics of the festival continue — the cooking, the rituals, the necklaces being clasped, the chai being made — and life happens in the margins of those mechanics.
Real life does not pause for its own drama. A couple can be in the middle of a fight that has been ten years in the making and still need to figure out who is picking up the cousin from the station.
That is the film's central and most beautiful observation. A wife is disappointed and angry at her husband's choices — and she voices her disappointment while also asking him to help her put her necklace on. The argument does not stop. The necklace still needs to be fastened. Both things are true at the same time. Ghosh is one of the very few filmmakers who trusts that this is enough. That the gap between the necklace and the argument is where actual life lives.
The craft
What Ghosh does that others don't
The concept is not new. Ekannoborti barir Durga Pujo has been the backdrop of Bengali drama for generations. Family members returning to the old house, old wounds reopening, secrets surfacing — Ghosh is working with material that Bengali audiences know in their bones. What he does differently is refuse to turn any of it into a moment.
There are no confrontations in this film that feel like confrontations. There are no revelations that arrive with the visual grammar of a revelation. He uses long takes, slow pans, deceptive cuts, an almost absent background score. He trusts his actors and his dialogue the way a confident writer trusts a sentence — without decoration. Simple words articulating complex feelings about adulthood, about the things you thought you'd have figured out by now, about loving people you have also disappointed.
I
When Asit finally tells his elder brother about his financial trouble, he does it while sharing a cigarette — a local one, not even a proper cigarette — on the side of the house where nobody can hear them. The disclosure is enormous. The setting is entirely ordinary. That gap is where Ghosh lives.
II
Arun's anger at the Bengali tendency toward apolitical detachment. His disillusionment at politics in general. Voiced not in a speech but as a throwaway comment, the kind of thing you say while everyone else is busy with something else. These are the minute flourishes that elevate Utsab above the rest.
III
A married couple mid-argument, mid-chore. The conflict between them is real and old. The celebrations continue around them. The deity blesses the house. The joy of the occasion strains to hold everything together, almost breaking at the seams — but not quite.
A line from the film, quoted by a reviewer who could not stop thinking about it
Prosenjit: "তোমার চা — ফ্রেঞ্চ টোস্ট ভালো হয়েছিল।"
Rituparna: "এইটা বলবার জন্যে বুঝি এতদূর তেল পুড়িয়ে এলে?"
Prosenjit: "হ্যাঁ, খুশি?"
There is no subtext being hammered in. No lesson. Just two people, years of something between them, and a question about whether the other person is happy. Ghosh understood that this is what passes for tenderness between people who have been to the wars of a long relationship and survived. Small gestures. Unnecessary journeys. Tea and French toast.
On watching it
Who this film is for — and who it isn't
One reviewer called it a comfort film. Another called it sombre. Both are right, and neither is a contradiction.
This film is not for everyone. The people who say this are not being elitist — they are being accurate. If you have never sat in a room where two relatives speak very carefully to each other because thirty years of a particular kind of love and resentment are both present in every sentence, you may find this slow. You may find it undramatic. You may wonder why everyone is so polite when they are clearly furious.
But if you have sat in that room — if you have been the cousin watching through a half-open door, or the aunt who knows more than she is saying, or the son trying to calculate how to tell his father something that will disappoint him — then this film will feel less like watching a story and more like watching your own life at a slight remove. Recognizable but just far enough away to bear looking at.
A reviewer who grew up in a Bengali joint family of 27
"I could deeply feel and relate to the relationships, the bonding, the cousins, uncles, aunts — all those sweet connections. And no one in the family wishes ill for another. There's no jealousy, no rivalry. Everyone just wants to stand by each other and work through the situation together."
That last observation — no jealousy, no rivalry, just people trying — is what separates Utsab from almost everything in this genre. The family in this film is not dysfunctional. It is functional in the messy, imperfect, very human way that actual families are functional. They are not falling apart because they are bad people. They are struggling because life is hard and they are trying to do it together and nobody told them this was going to require so much of them.

Performance
Prosenjit, and what Ghosh knew about him
Prosenjit, long before his mid-career reinvention, gives a performance here that reveals exactly who he could be in the right hands. Ghosh knew this. Ghosh spent much of his career knowing things about actors that the actors hadn't quite discovered about themselves yet. There is no cleverness, no subversion, no showboating. Just a man, inside a family, carrying something he doesn't know how to put down.
Rituparna Sengupta and Mamata Shankar, both central to the film, occasionally slip into a pitch that belongs to a different kind of Bengali cinema — the commercial loudness, the theatrical declaration. In a film built almost entirely on understatement, these moments jar. They are not fatal — both pull it back — but they are noticeable. In a Ghosh film, where every other thing is tuned so precisely, the inconsistency registers more than it would elsewhere.
The resolution
An ending that doesn't resolve — it accepts
The film does not end with resolution in any conventional sense. It ends with acceptance. With people returning, imperfectly, to what they know to be right, even when right is not easy or happy or what they would have chosen in another life. One reviewer hated this. Another found it to be the most honest thing the film could have done. Both responses make complete sense.
The film earns the ending it has because it spent its entire length showing you that none of these people are good or bad. They are simply people, carrying what people carry, doing the best that people do — which is usually just enough and sometimes not quite.
Final word
Ghosh understood that a family is not held together by its grand moments but by the accumulation of its ordinary ones — the breakfasts, the shared cigarettes, the small rituals of noticing each other that happen without announcement and are only understood as love in retrospect.
