The player who was always almost everything
There is a moment from January 2010 that I keep coming back to. Real Madrid are away at Deportivo. The match is already won. Guti finds himself one on one with the goalkeeper. Everything in the logic of football says: shoot. Instead, he back-heels it.

Not to a teammate standing next to him. He back-heels it sideways, across the edge of the six-yard box, completely fooling the goalkeeper who had committed to diving the wrong way, into the path of Karim Benzema who taps into an empty net. The goalkeeper ends up on the ground, looking at the ceiling, trying to work out what just happened.
Marca called it the "back-heel of God."
That single moment contains everything you need to know about Guti. The absurd talent. The refusal to do what he was supposed to do. And the fact that it came in a nothing league match against a mid-table side rather than a Champions League final. He gave you those moments often. He just never gave them to you when the whole world was watching. And somehow that is both the tragedy and the entire point.
01
The boy who stayed
José María Gutiérrez Hernández joined Real Madrid's academy when he was eight years old. Eight. He would spend the next 25 years there, growing up inside the walls of the biggest football club on the planet.
He was a striker in the youth teams, then a playmaker, then a midfielder, then whatever the team needed him to be at any given moment. When Clarence Seedorf arrived from Sampdoria in 1996, Guti found himself on the bench. He was 19. He had just started to break through.
He had already scored in a European Championship final at youth level. The opposition that day featured Buffon, Pirlo, Totti and Ambrosini. He was good enough to do that. He was clearly, visibly, obviously good enough.
But good enough and Real Madrid are not always the same thing.
02
The eternal promise
Former president Ramón Calderón gave him a label that would follow him for the rest of his career. He called Guti the "eternal promise." He meant it as a compliment. He had no idea it would become a kind of verdict.
He is 31 years old and people are still talking about his potential.
Ramón Calderón, Real Madrid president
There is something quietly devastating about that sentence. By the time Calderón said it, Guti had already won three Champions Leagues, made over 300 appearances for Real Madrid, and played in some of the most talented squads in football history. And he was still being described as potential. As something that had not quite arrived yet. The cruelest part is that it was true. Even the people who loved him most could not say with certainty that the player they occasionally glimpsed on the pitch had ever actually shown up and stayed.
03
Guti and the Galácticos
There is a quote from Guti in 2003 that once you read it you cannot unhear it.
All the doors are closing on me. I was improving as a midfielder and Zidane arrived. I was improving as a forward and Ronaldo arrived. I'm now in the national team as a midfielder and Beckham comes.
Guti · June 2003
Zidane. Ronaldo. Beckham. Three of the most famous footballers in human history, arriving one after another to occupy precisely the space Guti was trying to claim. It would have broken most players. It would have sent most of them to a different club, a different country, a different life.
Guti stayed. Every single time.
One manager remarked on arriving at the club: "Look at all the photographs on the walls here of all the great successes. Guti is not in any of the pictures." He was there for all of it. He won trophies as a core contributor. He lasted through 19 different managers. And somehow, he was still the armchair you moved around to make room for the new leather sofas.
That kind of loyalty is either the most admirable thing in football or the saddest. In Guti's case it was genuinely both at the same time.

04
What he actually was
When you strip away the Galáctico noise, what you are left with is a player of extraordinary, almost embarrassing natural ability. Ronaldo, the Brazilian one, the man who won the World Cup twice and was probably the best striker who ever lived, called Guti the finest talent in the squad during his time in Madrid.
Think about what that means. Ronaldo, standing in a dressing room that contained Zidane, Figo, Beckham, Roberto Carlos and Raúl, looked around and pointed at the quiet Spaniard with the long hair and said: him. That one.
Wesley Sneijder said Guti was the best player he ever shared a pitch with. He also said that when he first arrived at Madrid and took Guti's place in the starting eleven, Guti did not speak to him for three months. They eventually made up and played brilliantly together. Complicated, yes. But not petty forever.
His passing was what people ran out of adjectives trying to describe. The range of it, the disguise of it, the way he could thread a ball through spaces that other players had not yet noticed existed. He played football at a slightly different angle to everyone else, as if he was seeing the pitch from slightly higher up, slightly further forward in time.
05
Forty-five minutes
When Zidane retired in 2006, Fabio Capello said he wanted to build the team around Guti. This was it. This was the moment the Galácticos were leaving one by one, and Guti, the boy who had waited patiently through all of them, was finally going to be the main character.
Capello quickly discovered that the player he saw in those flashes from the bench was not quite the same player who showed up when handed a starting role and the full weight of expectation. In small doses, coming off the bench when the game needed something different, Guti was extraordinary. As the permanent solution, the man you built your team around, he was something else. Something less.
The Sevilla match in 2006/07 is the clearest example. Madrid losing 1-0 at home at half time. Capello sends Guti on in place of Raúl, hands him the captain's armband. Less than 20 minutes later Guti has two brilliant assists, total authority, the absolute best version of himself. Madrid win 3-1.
He needed to be losing. He needed to be coming on. Start him, and you got a different person.
The emergency version of Guti was one of the most exciting players in the world. The everyday version was the one that drove every manager he ever had to distraction.
06
After the beach
When he left for Beşiktaş in 2010, he said he was going to spend the rest of his life on a beach in Thailand. Everyone believed him, because it fit. The playboy, the maverick, the man who had never quite committed to anything the way his talent demanded.
Then he took his coaching badges. Went back to Real Madrid. Started working with the youth teams. Won a youth treble. The kids he worked with, Federico Valverde among them, speak about him with a specific kind of reverence. He understood something about young talent and what it feels like to be the eternal promise.
Maybe because he had been that player. Maybe because he understood from the inside exactly what it cost.
He is José María Gutiérrez Hernández now in his professional life. The full name. The adult name. The name of someone who has decided to be taken seriously.
But Guti is the one we remember. The long hair. The back-heel. The 45 minutes against Sevilla that briefly made everyone believe this was finally it. It was never finally it. It was always just glimpses. And the glimpses, it turns out, were enough to last a lifetime.

The temperature of the man
He needed friction to be brilliant.

He kicked players who were down. He spat at opponents. He turned up two hours late to training with an explanation about his phone and the clocks that did not add up.

He publicly resented the coverage given to Beckham. He was asked to leave by Mourinho, and nobody was surprised.

And yet. Every player who played alongside him speaks about him with something close to reverence. Both versions were true at the same time.

Final word
He scored Madrid's 5,000th league goal. Their 500th Champions League goal. The eternal promise never quite became the finished article. The glimpses were enough to last a lifetime.
It was never finally it. It was always just glimpses. And somehow, that was the whole point.
