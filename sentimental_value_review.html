Film review · 2025
Sentimental Value
Directed by Joachim Trier
A Stellan Skarsgård Renate Reinsve Elle Fanning
An aging filmmaker, Gustav Borg, tries to re-enter his daughter Nora's life after her mother's death — not with an apology, but with a film. He casts her not as herself, but as his own mother, whose suicide is the wound he has never addressed. It's a gesture that blurs the line between intimacy and exploitation, and the film never fully lets him off the hook for it.
The central tension
Gustav
Seeks understanding, not forgiveness. Uses the film as a vehicle to communicate what he cannot say in person. Not overtly cruel — passively harmful, existing one step removed from the damage he causes.
Nora
Rejects both the film and the emotional logic behind it. Torn between resentment and unresolved attachment. Her stage fright isn't professional — it's an identity crisis. She became an actress partly to avoid being herself.
Key themes
Mercy vs forgiveness
The film draws a careful line: mercy is allowance, a performance of goodwill. Forgiveness is absolution, a final act. Trier argues reconciliation can only begin with the former — and may never reach the latter.
Art as communication
Gustav uses cinema to articulate what direct speech fails to carry. The film-within-the-film becomes a bridge — and a trap — between an unresolved past and a present that keeps reopening it.
Generational trauma
Gustav's mother's suicide isn't backstory — it's architecture. He doesn't originate the pain; he conducts it. Trauma here is inherited and passed forward, not resolved.
Identity and performance
Nora is made to watch another actress — Rachel — perform a version of her own life. The dissonance between lived experience and performed identity is where the film finds its sharpest emotional edges.
Character breakdown
Gustav Borg
Stellan Skarsgård
Seeks meaning in past trauma rather than accountability. His intelligence and talent make his emotional absence harder to forgive, not easier.
Nora
Renate Reinsve
The emotional centre. Her arc isn't about healing — it's about surviving proximity to someone who uses love as raw material. Reinsve holds the whole film together.
Rachel Kemp
Elle Fanning
A Hollywood actress seeking artistic credibility, cast in Nora's role. She represents outside intrusion into a private wound — an unwitting instrument of someone else's story.
The house as character
The ancestral Oslo home absorbs everything — grief, memory, the weight of unspoken things. It isn't a neutral setting. Its rooms hold emotional residue the way old wood holds damp. The physical structure of the house mirrors the Borg family's psychological one: load-bearing walls where there should be open doors.

Form and style
Structure
Non-linear, layered. Moves between present tensions, family history research, and film-within-film sequences without losing coherence. Blackouts and deliberate pacing give it a stage-like rhythm.
Tone
Emotionally rich but never overwrought. Blends quiet humor with deep psychological tension — the laughs land precisely because everything around them is heavy.
Visual texture
Tactile, lived-in spaces. The cinematography favors surfaces with history — worn wood, aged paper, the grain of a room that has been inhabited for decades.
"The ending is predictable in structure — and overwhelming in execution."
Emotional payoff derives from accumulated detail, not surprise. The convergence of past, present, and memory lands because the film has earned every second of it.
Meta-cinema
Sentimental Value is genuinely rare among films-about-filmmaking: it earns the reflexivity. Gustav's past work is used to establish both his brilliance and his decline — not as decoration, but as evidence. The film critiques the artistic impulse without dismissing it. Art can reveal truth, Trier suggests, but it can also distort it, especially when the artist is both subject and author.
Context
Comparable to Aftersun
Similar in emotional register — what's withheld does more work than what's shown. Both films weaponize the gap between a parent's inner life and what a child can access.
Peak Trier
Functions as a culmination of his career's recurring themes — grief, identity, Oslo — but expanded from individual interiority to full family portrait. His most structurally ambitious film.
A deeply layered family drama that uses filmmaking as both subject and method. It dissects memory, identity, and inherited pain with rare precision — and arrives at something honest: love is limited by one's ability to express it. Healing is not resolution. It is recognition. Understanding requires performance before it can become authenticity.
