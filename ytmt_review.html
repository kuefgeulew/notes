Gael García Bernal Diego Luna Maribel Verdú Written by the Cuarón brothers
reviewed alongside —
Before Sunrise (1995) Call Me By Your Name (2017) Moonlight (2016) Boyhood (2014) Roma (2018)
He did not expect to feel this much. That is the first thing worth saying about Y Tu Mamá También. You sit down thinking it is a film about two teenage boys being horny on a road trip, and it is that, it is fully and unapologetically that, but then somewhere along the way the road opens up and the camera drifts outside the car window and you realize you are watching something much heavier than you signed up for.
The story is simple on the surface. Julio and Tenoch are seventeen, bored, loud, and incapable of thinking about anything except sex. Their girlfriends leave for Italy for the summer. At a wedding, they meet Luisa, the wife of a cousin, older and beautiful and clearly carrying something the boys cannot yet name. They invite her on a made-up road trip to a beach they have invented on the spot. She says no. Then her husband calls and confesses he has been cheating, and she calls them back and says yes.
So the three of them get in a car and drive across Mexico.
Before Sunrise came six years before this film and it understood something similar about roads and time. Jesse and Celine have one night in Vienna and they spend it walking and talking and falling into something they cannot name before the morning takes it away. The journey is everything. The destination is almost beside the point. Y Tu Mamá También knows this too, but where Before Sunrise is careful and tender and built from words, this film is built from bodies and movement and the things people refuse to say. Both films understand that travel removes you from your ordinary life long enough that you forget to perform yourself. And in that gap, something true gets in.
What follows is one of the most honest films ever made about being young. Not honest in the polished, careful way that most coming-of-age films try to be. Honest in the way that is sometimes embarrassing to watch because it is too close to something true. The boys finish too quickly. They argue over nothing. They have a manifesto with rules they immediately break. They talk about sex the way people talk about things they do not actually understand yet, loudly and with great confidence.
Early in the film, in one of those brash unguarded exchanges that only teenagers have without embarrassment, Tenoch says something like, I fucked Ceci a few times. Julio responds, No big deal. I poked Ana a bunch of times. It is a throwaway exchange. Two boys one-upping each other. But it tells you everything about where they are. They are at the age where saying a thing out loud is the same as understanding it. Where the vocabulary of sex has arrived before the emotional comprehension has. The film never mocks them for this. It watches them the way the narrator watches everything, with patience and a kind of affection that has no illusions.
And underneath all of it, they are in love with each other. Not in a way either of them can say out loud. Not in a way the film forces onto the screen. But it is there in every scene, in the way they move around each other, in the rules they make about what they are allowed to do and who they are allowed to touch.
Moonlight covered this same territory fifteen years later with an entirely different register. Chiron's desire is buried under survival, under violence, under a world that has no space for what he feels. The suppression in Moonlight is active and brutal. In Y Tu Mamá También the suppression is almost unconscious. Julio and Tenoch are not suffering. They are having the time of their lives. And yet the rules they make, the constant need to assert themselves, the particular discomfort when conversations get too close — it is all performance. Both films arrive at the same truth from completely different directions. The things people most need to say are the things they build the most elaborate systems to avoid saying.
Call Me By Your Name took a more romantic approach to this same silence. Elio and Oliver circle each other through an Italian summer, and the film gives them a tenderness that Julio and Tenoch never quite get. Elio's father delivers a speech near the end about not suppressing what you feel, about the cost of closing yourself off. It is the speech this film never gives anyone. Cuarón is not interested in comfort. He does not want his characters to be told what to do with what they feel. He wants to show you what happens when nobody says the speech, when the summer ends and the boys go back to their separate lives and take the unsaid thing with them permanently.
* * *
On Luisa. On time. On giving yourself to the sea.
Luisa is the one who holds it together for a while. She is not just the older woman who teaches the boys about sex. She is someone in the middle of her own reckoning. She has just found out her husband has been unfaithful. She is carrying something the audience does not fully understand until near the end. She gets in the car not because of the boys but because she needs to go somewhere, and the boys happen to have a car.
At some point during the trip, Luisa asks the boys whether they would want to live forever. It is a question that seems casual in the moment. She asks it with the lightness of someone making conversation. But knowing what we know by the end, the question does not feel casual at all. She is the only one of the three who knows exactly how much time is worth. The boys laugh it off.
"
La vida es como la espuma, por eso hay que darse como el mar.
Life is like the surf, so give yourself away like the sea.
The boys say this like a motto, like something cool they invented. When Luisa says it, it means something different. She is the only one who is actually doing it. She is giving herself to whatever comes next without resistance or bitterness. She floats in the ocean at Heaven's Mouth and she smiles. Not because everything is fine. Because she has decided to let things be what they are.
Boyhood did something similar with time, though from a completely different angle. Linklater filmed his characters over twelve actual years, and the result was that you watched a boy age in real time on screen. The emotional weight came from accumulation, from small moments adding up into a life. Y Tu Mamá También compresses that same accumulation into a single road trip. The months between the beginning and the coffee shop at the end feel like years. The boys who get in that car at the start are not quite the same people who sit across from each other in silence at the end, and the film makes you feel that change without explaining it.
* * *
The narrator. The country outside the window. The margins that become the point.
The narrator is one of the strangest choices in the film and also one of the best. Every so often the sound drops out and a calm, detached voice tells you something about a character that the character will never know. A small detail, a piece of context, something that happened before or will happen after. At one point, about a small roadside shrine that the boys drive past without looking, the narrator pauses the entire film to explain the story behind it. A man who died there. A family left behind. The country continuing its life around these three people who are too young to notice.
Roma becomes unavoidable here. Cuarón returned to Mexico nearly two decades later with a film that did almost exactly the opposite. Where Y Tu Mamá También follows two boys who cannot see past their own immediate desires, Roma centers on Cleo, a domestic worker whose quiet suffering exists in the background of a wealthy family that barely sees her. Both films hold class at arm's length from the main story and let it accumulate in the margins. The difference is that in Y Tu Mamá También the boys are on the privileged side of that distance. In Roma, Cuarón closes it entirely. He had learned by then how to make the margins the center.
There is a line from the manifesto the boys wrote for themselves, something about always telling each other the truth.
"
La verdad es lo mejor, pero es dificilísimo de alcanzar.
The truth is the best thing, but it is very hard to reach.
They wrote this rule as teenagers, the way teenagers write rules, believing that making a thing a rule is the same as living by it. By the end of the film every significant truth between them has gone unsaid. They slept with each other's girlfriends and said nothing for months. They kissed each other in the night and pretended it did not happen. The rule about truth was the rule they broke most completely.
* * *
The threesome. The ending. What does not survive.
The threesome near the end, when it finally happens, is not the celebration it sounds like. It is the moment everything breaks. Two boys who have been performing a version of themselves for each other their entire lives finally close the distance they were never supposed to close. The next morning neither of them can look at the other. The friendship, which was the whole point, does not survive.
This is where the film diverges sharply from Call Me By Your Name, which allows its characters a long and painful grief after the summer ends. Elio cries by the fireplace and the film holds that cry for as long as it needs to. Y Tu Mamá También does not give the boys a fireplace or a long cry. It gives them a coffee shop and a brief conversation and the narrator telling you afterward that they never met again. Most endings are not even recognized as endings until much later.
The ending is quiet and devastating. They meet at a coffee shop months later. One of them mentions that Luisa died not long after they returned. The other does not know what to say. They go their separate ways. The narrator tells you this the way it tells you everything, without drama, as a simple fact.
Every film mentioned here, Before Sunrise, Call Me By Your Name, Moonlight, Boyhood, Roma, they all know this feeling. They all found different ways into it. But Y Tu Mamá También found it first among this particular group, and in some ways it found it most honestly, because it was not trying to be beautiful. It was just trying to be true.
She was the only one of the three who actually managed to give herself away like the sea. The boys were too young and too afraid. They will spend years becoming people who are less free than they were in that car, on that road, heading toward a beach that barely existed.
He did not expect to feel this much.
But that is exactly what the film was counting on.
