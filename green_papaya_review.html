Personal review · 2025
First encounter
A film that refuses
to be followed

The Scent of Green Papaya is difficult to approach on a first viewing because it quietly rejects the expectations you bring into it. It opens like it might unfold into a conventional narrative set in Vietnam, but that assumption collapses quickly. For a while, that creates friction — the early stretch feels almost disorienting, because the film refuses to supply the usual structure of plot, conflict, or progression.

That confusion only resolves once the expectation of "story" is abandoned. The film is not built around events but around presence. It operates on an experiential level, asking to be observed rather than followed. There are no dramatic climaxes, no sharp emotional pivots, no narrative urgency. What exists instead is a quiet, continuous attention to life.

"The absence of plot stops being a limitation and becomes the film's defining strength."
On the shift in perception
The shift happens gradually. By the time the film moves forward in time, the resistance fades and the rhythm starts to take hold. What initially felt empty begins to feel deliberate. It creates space — for texture, for stillness, for detail that has no obligation to mean anything beyond itself.
Three Readings
The same film — seen differently
I
Analytical
The film is closer to watching moments accumulate than watching a story unfold. Each scene feels like a fragment — small, self-contained, and delicate. Over time, those fragments form something cohesive, but not in a way that can be easily summarized. It's less about what happens and more about what is felt. On a first watch, it can be frustrating if approached with conventional expectations. Once those are stripped away, it becomes absorbing in a different way — calm, patient, deeply attentive.
Observation · Process · Patience
II
Lyrical
A sensory experience that demands the audience to get intoxicated by its textures and aromas while guiding them with elegant poetry through the fine tapestry of life. It is a small tribute to the Vietnamese woman — like fresh seed taken from a fruit, growing pure and shining with great splendor. A poem that evokes happiness. A contemplation of an existence that develops with serenity, in a world unknown to many of us.
Sensation · Poetry · Light
III
Critical
The proficiency of Anh Hùng's craft makes us question whether what we're watching is worth being observed with such beauty in the first place. A caged cricket at peace with its existence — but we can't attribute our own feelings to the near-wordless life of the person actually enduring it. Whether the director approves of the culturally enforced subjugation of his lead never seems to enter the equation, and that's largely what gives the film its indescribable, troubling allure.
Complicity · Power · Gaze
IV
The Craft
The world feels natural and lived-in, yet it is meticulously constructed — all shot entirely on a French soundstage.
What the camera does
—
Image as language
The close-ups — papaya sap dripping, water trembling in a basin, light shifting across surfaces — are not decorative. They are the film's language. The camera lingers, drifts, observes. Each texture carries weight the dialogue never needs to carry.
—
The voyeuristic frame
Benoît Delhomme's camera slides along from the vantage point of a windowsill or open doorway, augmenting the impression that we are simply outside observers to a life we cannot judge. There is an almost Spielbergian control to the motion — each smooth glide makes way for a potential shift in subject.
—
Inhabited space
The homes — filled with greenery, objects, quiet rituals — are presented with such care that they begin to feel inhabited beyond the characters. The camera doesn't just show the environment; it invites you into it. You stop watching and start existing alongside it.
—
Score and silence
Combined with the restrained, melancholic score, it builds a form of mood cinema that is immersive without ever being forceful. The music arrives not to underscore emotion but to deepen the quality of air in the room.
Final reading
"Its impact is quiet but lasting, built entirely on image, sound, and the discipline of restraint."
There is technically a romantic thread, but it arrives late and functions more as a quiet extension of the film's tone than its driving force. It doesn't reframe the film into a narrative; it simply adds another layer to the atmosphere. The experience resists summary because it was never built to be summarized. Each viewing deposits something different — a texture noticed, a silence felt more deeply. This is a film that becomes more itself the less you ask of it.
